1) CORE IMAGE INTENSITY TRANSFORMATIONS
==================================================

These operate on pixel values directly.

1.1 Negative transformation
Formula:
s = L - 1 - r
For 8-bit images: s = 255 - r

Effect:
- Dark pixels become bright.
- Bright pixels become dark.
- Histogram is mirrored around the center.

Best for:
- Medical/remote sensing style images where bright background inversion helps.
- Highlighting details in dark regions.
- Visual inspection when object/background contrast is low.

Not ideal for:
- Natural photos where inversion looks unnatural.
- Images where semantic meaning depends on original polarity.

- Negative does not ŌĆ£remove noiseŌĆØ; it only flips intensities.

1.2 Log transformation
Formula:
s = c * log(1 + r)

Purpose:
- Compresses dynamic range.
- Expands dark regions.
- Compresses very bright values.

Why it works:
- The log curve rises steeply at low intensities and flattens at high intensities.
- So dark details get stretched more than bright details.

Effect of c:
- Larger c increases overall brightness and stretch.
- Too large c can saturate values and wash out highlights.
- Smaller c gives a milder effect.

Brightens or darkens?
- Usually brightens dark areas.
- Overall output may look brighter if c is large enough.

Best for:
- Dark images.
- Images with bright highlights and hidden shadow details.
- Dynamic range compression.

Pitfall:
- Can over-amplify noise in dark regions if the image is very noisy.

- Expand a narrow intensity range to a wider range.

- Images that look flat or washed out

2) FILTERS FOR NOISE
==================================================

2.1 Mean filter / averaging filter
What it does:
- Replaces each pixel by the average of neighboring pixels.

Best for:
- Gaussian noise
- Small random fluctuations

Effect:
- Smooths the image.
- Reduces high-frequency noise.
- Blurs edges.

Kernel examples:
- 3x3 mean filter
- 5x5 mean filter

Kernel size rule:
- Bigger kernel = more smoothing = more blur.

Not ideal for:
- Salt-and-pepper noise
- Edge preservation

Referenced in Lab 3. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file2Ņłü


2.2 Gaussian filter
What it does:
- Weighted averaging where center pixels matter more than far pixels.

Best for:
- Gaussian noise
- Natural smoothing
- Preprocessing before edge detection

Effect:
- Less harsh blur than a plain mean filter.
- Preserves structures slightly better than mean filter.

Sigma rule:
- Small sigma = mild smoothing
- Large sigma = stronger smoothing and more blur

Kernel size rule:
- Larger kernel generally means more smoothing.

Referenced in Lab 3. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file2Ņłü


2.3 Median filter
What it does:
- Replaces a pixel by the median of neighborhood values.

Best for:
- Salt-and-pepper noise
- Impulse noise

Why it works:
- Outliers like 0 and 255 do not dominate the median.

Effect:
- Removes isolated spikes very well.
- Preserves edges better than mean filter.

Why it is not convolution:
- It is nonlinear.
- It does not compute a weighted sum.

Kernel size rule:
- Bigger window removes more noise but can remove detail.

Referenced in Lab 3. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file2Ņłü


2.4 Min filter
What it does:
- Replaces each pixel with the minimum value in the neighborhood.

Best for:
- Removing bright impulse noise
- Expanding dark regions
- Erosion-like behavior

Effect:
- Darkens image.
- Shrinks bright objects.
- Useful when noise appears as bright specks.

Not ideal for:
- General smoothing
- Preserving bright detail

Exam clue:
- Min filter is like a strong darkening morphological effect.

2.5 Max filter
What it does:
- Replaces each pixel with the maximum value in the neighborhood.

Best for:
- Removing dark impulse noise
- Expanding bright regions
- Dilation-like behavior

Effect:
- Brightens image.
- Enlarges bright objects.
- Useful when noise appears as black specks.

2.6 Contraharmonic mean filter
Formula:
g = sum(f^(Q+1)) / sum(f^Q)

Where Q controls the behavior.

Important Q rules:
- Q > 0  ŌåÆ good for salt noise (bright impulse noise)
- Q < 0  ŌåÆ good for pepper noise (dark impulse noise)
- Q = 0  ŌåÆ becomes arithmetic mean
- Q = -1 ŌåÆ harmonic mean
- Larger positive Q gives more emphasis to large values
- More negative Q gives more emphasis to small values

Practical interpretation:
- Use positive Q when image has white dots.
- Use negative Q when image has black dots.

Important caution:
- It is sensitive to zeros when Q is negative.
- Can fail or behave badly if the denominator becomes unstable.

This is one of the most important exam filters for impulse noise.

2.7 Alpha-trimmed mean filter
Idea:
- Sort values in the neighborhood.
- Remove the lowest d/2 and highest d/2 values.
- Average the remaining values.

Purpose:
- Combines mean filter and median-like robustness.

Best for:
- Mixed noise
- When both Gaussian-like noise and impulse noise are present

Effect:
- Less sensitive to extreme outliers than mean filter.
- Less aggressive than median if alpha is small.

Parameter rule:
- Larger alpha trim removes more extremes.
- Too much trimming may remove detail.

Exam memory:
- Mean = smoothest but weakest against outliers.
- Median = strongest against impulses.
- Alpha-trim = compromise.
- Contraharmonic = directional impulse-noise handling using Q.

4.1 Erosion

10) IMPORTANT HISTOGRAM FACTS

Negative:

Log:
- Dark bins spread out.
- Bright bins compress.

Gamma < 1:

==================================================
11) COLOR IMAGE WARNING
==================================================

Applying intensity transforms directly to RGB channels can distort color balance.

Better idea:
- Convert to HSV or LAB.
- Apply transform to the intensity-like channel only.

Reason:
- Human vision cares more about luminance than raw RGB channel-wise transforms.
